Local Food

Malaysian cuisine has a long history, deeply influenced by Malay, Chinese, and Indian cultures.

Blending spices, culinary techniques, and traditions, dishes like Nasi Lemak and Laksa embody the nation's heritage.

Food has become an inseparable part of Malaysian culture, reflecting its rich diversity and identity.

Food introduction and their recipe

NASI LEMAK

Nasi lemak originated in Malaysia as a traditional Malay dish. It was initially consumed by Malay farmers and fishermen because the coconut milk-infused rice provided a good source of energy. Originally, nasi lemak was served with simple sides like anchovies, cucumber, eggs, and sambal. Over time, it evolved to include additional toppings such as fried chicken and beef rendang. Today, it is a popular and iconic dish in Malaysia, Singapore, and Indonesia.

ASAM LAKSA

Asam Laksa is a famous Malaysian dish, originating from Penang. It features a tangy, fish-based broth flavored with tamarind, lemongrass, and chili, served with rice noodles. Influenced by Malay and Chinese culinary traditions, it evolved through cultural exchanges in Malaysia’s trading ports. Today, Asam Laksa is a beloved street food, known for its bold flavors and global recognition.

Learn how to cook it




SATAY

Satay is a popular Southeast Asian dish, believed to have originated in Indonesia and later spread to Malaysia and beyond. It consists of skewered and grilled meat, usually served with a rich peanut sauce, rice cakes, and cucumber. Influenced by Middle Eastern kebabs, satay became a staple street food, known for its smoky aroma and flavorful marinade.

Learn how to cook it




CHAR KWAY TEOW

Char Kway Teow originated in Malaysia, especially in Penang, as a cheap and filling meal for laborers. Street vendors stir-fried rice noodles with prawns, eggs, and soy sauce over high heat, creating the dish’s signature smoky flavor. Over time, it evolved from humble street food into a national favorite, enjoyed across Malaysia.

Learn how to cook it




ROTI CANAI

Roti Canai was introduced to Malaysia by Indian immigrants, particularly from Tamil Nadu. Originally a simple flatbread, it became a staple street food, often served with dhal or curry. Over time, variations like roti telur (with egg) and roti boom (thicker and sweeter) emerged, making it a beloved dish nationwide.

Learn how to cook it




BAK KUT TEH

Bak Kut Teh originated from Chinese immigrants in Malaysia, particularly in Klang. It was created as a hearty, herbal pork rib soup to nourish laborers working in ports and tin mines. Over time, different variations emerged, including Teochew (lighter broth) and Hokkien (darker, stronger flavors), making it a beloved dish across Southeast Asia.
